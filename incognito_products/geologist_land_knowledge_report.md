# The Geologist and the Ground Beneath: Land Knowledge, Extraction, and Power in World Cinema

*A cultural-geographic reading of films featuring geologist characters*

---

## Framing the Figure

The geologist is cinema's most ambivalent reader of the earth. Unlike the miner, the engineer, or the colonial administrator, the geologist's labor is epistemic before it is extractive: they produce *knowledge* of the subsurface — what lies beneath, what it is worth, whether it will hold. But that knowledge is never neutral. To map a diamond field, to declare a slope unstable, to certify a valley as dammable is to convert the lived ground of others into a legible, transferable, and ultimately *appropriable* resource. The geologist stands at the hinge between seeing and taking.

This report reads sixteen films across the corpus through four questions: **Who owns the knowledge of the land? Who benefits? What binds geological expertise to extraction, displacement, and colonial power? And how does each film spatialize the collision between scientific and Indigenous or local ways of knowing the earth?**

Three broad clusters emerge, and the films sort themselves into them with surprising clarity:

1. **The geologist as agent of the extractive-colonial state** — knowledge as the leading edge of dispossession.
2. **The geologist as cassandra against capital** — expert warning ignored by profit or the state.
3. **The geologist as romantic decipherer of deep time** — the documentary and adventure mode, where the land becomes a text with no human owner, a framing that quietly *empties* the ground of prior claimants.

## Cluster One: Expertise as the Edge of the Plow

### *Where the Green Ants Dream* (Werner Herzog, Australia/Germany, 1984)

This is the corpus's keystone text and the one that states the central conflict most baldly. A uranium mining company sends surveyors and geologists onto Aboriginal land; the company "wants to tear open the soil and search for uranium," while the Aboriginal people hold that this is the place "where the green ants go to dream," and that disturbing their dreaming will "bring down disaster on us all." The film stages an absolute incommensurability between two cartographies of the same dirt. For the company's science, the ground is depth, ore grade, and overburden — a vertical, value-bearing column. For the Aboriginal custodians, the ground is a horizontal field of relation, story, and ancestral presence that cannot be priced because it cannot be alienated.

**Who owns the knowledge?** The geologist's knowledge is *portable* — it travels to a courtroom, a boardroom, a national capital, and there it is recognized as knowledge. Indigenous knowledge, by contrast, is rendered as "belief," admissible only as ethnographic curiosity. The film's tragedy is precisely this asymmetry of epistemic standing: one way of knowing the earth is granted the force of law and capital; the other must perform itself in an alien legal grammar that guarantees its defeat. Geological expertise here is not merely *adjacent* to colonial power — it is its sensory organ, the instrument by which the settler state perceives land as empty, available, and beneath (literally) the people who live on it.

### *Papaya: Love Goddess of the Cannibals* (Italy, 1978)

Stripped of its exploitation-genre packaging, the plot is a remarkably undisguised allegory of extractive colonialism: a team of geologists arrives to "remove a native population from an island to perform atomic research." Displacement is not a side effect here — it is the *prerequisite* of the science. The land must be cleared of its knowers before it can be made into a site of (nuclear) experiment. The film's revenge structure, in which the Indigenous leader "disposes of them one by one," is the colonial unconscious turned inside out: the land's people, figured as monstrous, reclaim agency over a territory the scientists assumed was theirs to evacuate. That the genre demonizes the Indigenous woman even as it grants her victory is itself the ideological knot worth naming — resistance is legible to the film only as horror.

### *Letter Never Sent* (Mikhail Kalatozov, Soviet Union, 1960)

Four geologists search for diamonds in the Siberian taiga, succeed, and "put the diamond mine on the map." This is the *socialist* face of the same act: extraction reframed as heroic service to the collective rather than to private capital. Yet the geographic logic is identical — the wilderness is a frontier, a void to be inscribed with value and delivered "back to Moscow." The map is the artifact that converts undifferentiated taiga into a national asset. The forest fire that traps and kills the surveyors reads, against the grain of its patriotic intent, as the land's own refusal — the taiga answering the act of mapping with annihilation. The Indigenous Siberian peoples whose territory this is appear nowhere; their absence is the film's most telling spatial fact.

### *Focusing on the Turquoise Mountain* (Czechoslovakia/Mongolia, 1978)

A comic register on the same theme: Mongolian-Czech geologists "discovering treasure deposits in Mongolia." The framing of mineral wealth as "treasure" to be "discovered" — as if it were unowned until found by the expert eye — is the durable colonial fiction, here laundered through socialist-bloc friendship and humor. The collaboration narrative softens but does not dissolve the underlying structure: expertise arrives from the metropole (Prague) to render the periphery's subsurface legible and productive.

### *A Place in the World* (Adolfo Aristarain, Argentina/Spain/Uruguay, 1992)

The most politically explicit film in the corpus about the *consequences* of geological knowledge. Hans, a "jaded Spanish geological engineer," surveys a remote Argentine valley "for the local patron, to see if it can be dammed for hydro-electric power, which would drive the peasants from the land into the cities." Here the geologist is the patron's instrument, and the film names the displacement plainly: the survey is the first act of an eviction. That Hans is Spanish — surveying Argentine land for an Argentine landowner — layers a second, postcolonial irony onto the scene. His expertise serves accumulation; the peasants' embodied knowledge of the valley counts for nothing against the hydrological calculation. The dam is the apparatus that would convert a lived valley into kilowatts and the people into urban labor.

## Cluster Two: The Geologist as Ignored Cassandra

A striking inversion runs through several films: the geologist who *knows* and is *not heard*. Here expertise is no longer the vanguard of capital but its conscience — overruled by it.

### *The Skin of the South* (Japan, 1952)

Young geologists declare a mountainside marked for residential development "unstable" and are scorned on two fronts: by villagers who refuse relocation and by a "headstrong lumber baron whose actions will only further destabilize the land." This is the corpus's most sophisticated spatial drama. The geologists possess accurate knowledge but no power; the lumber baron possesses power and an interest in *not* knowing; the villagers possess attachment to place that reads as obstinacy. Crucially, the villagers' resistance to relocation is *not* simple ignorance — it is the local knowledge of dwelling pitted against the abstract knowledge of risk. The film refuses to make the scientists straightforwardly heroic: their correct reading of the land is impotent precisely because they own neither the soil nor the means of acting on it. Knowledge without power "can do little but observe as nature runs its inevitable course."

### *The Wave* (Norway, 2015) and *The Quake* (Norway, 2018)

This Norwegian disaster diptych domesticates the cassandra structure for the global thriller market. In *The Wave*, a geologist monitoring the Geiranger fjord is "caught in the middle" when the predicted-but-unheeded mountain collapse generates a tsunami; in *The Quake*, the same figure "races against time" against an Oslo earthquake. The land here is not contested *between peoples* — it is a homogeneous national geo-hazard, and the geologist is recast as a familial protector. Notably, the politics of land ownership evaporate: there is no Indigenous claimant, no extractive antagonist, only nature as antagonist and the geologist as the lone individual who reads the warning signs the institution ignores. (That the Sámi — the Indigenous people of northern Norway — are absent from a film series obsessed with Norwegian land and its dangers is itself a spatial silence worth marking.) The genre converts a *political* problem of land knowledge into a *personal* problem of heroism.

### *Beyond Sleep* (Norway, 2016)

A young geologist seeking meteorite craters in the northern Norwegian tundra "gets hopelessly lost in both the wilderness and his own mind." This is the geologist's mastery turned inward and undone: the expert who comes to read the land is instead read — dissolved — by it. The tundra (again, Sámi country, again unnamed as such) refuses to be merely a field of data. The film's interest is psychological rather than political, but it inverts the extractive premise: the land is not a resource to be known but a force that exceeds and unmakes the knower.

## Cluster Three: Deep Time and the Emptied Ground

A large share of the corpus — especially the recent French documentary work — operates in a register where the geologist is neither colonizer nor cassandra but *interpreter of deep time*. This mode appears apolitical. It is not.

### *Great natural monuments — Grand Canyon* (France, 2025)

Geologists Karl Karlström and Laurie Crossey descend the Colorado to "unveil the mysteries" of "two billion years of the Earth's history" and "the place that humans occupy within it." The Grand Canyon is, of course, ancestral and present homeland to the Havasupai, Hopi, Navajo, Hualapai, and other nations — peoples for whom the canyon is not a geological text but a living relation, and whose dispossession from it is recent and ongoing. To frame the canyon as "two billion years of the Earth's history" read by two visiting experts is to perform a quiet erasure: the deep-time frame is so vast that it dwarfs and disappears the human-historical question of *whose land this is*. The film offers awe in place of accountability. Geological time becomes an alibi for political amnesia.

### *Great natural monuments — Iceland* (France/Switzerland, 2023)

Here the deep-time mode is least troubled, because Iceland genuinely had no Indigenous human population before Norse settlement — the land is "a natural laboratory" precisely because it can plausibly be figured as pre-human. This makes the film a useful control case: it shows what the deep-time documentary *wants* every landscape to be — an unowned text, a laboratory, a window onto origins. The contrast with the Grand Canyon film is instructive: the same epistemic frame that is honest in Iceland becomes an erasure when transposed onto inhabited, colonized ground.

### *The Living Stones of Sacsayhuamán* (Russia, 2014)

Russian geophysicists conduct soil research at the Inca citadel "at the request of Peru's Ministry of Culture," seeking to understand how its enormous stones were fitted. The film's premise — that "no living person knows how" the walls were built — is a familiar trope that subtly displaces Andean and Quechua knowledge: the assertion of mystery positions foreign scientific instruments as the path to understanding an Indigenous achievement, while the descendant communities who hold their own histories of the site are framed, by omission, as having lost the knowledge. The state (the Ministry) authorizes the foreign expert gaze over a heritage site; the geophysical survey becomes a new, technologized claim to interpret Indigenous land.

### *Voyages au centre de la Terre* (France, 2023) and *Big Bang in Tunguska* (Germany, 2008)

The Verne documentary literalizes the romantic-scientific imagination of the earth as an interior to be penetrated and narrated — the geologist as Vernean adventurer-hero. *Big Bang in Tunguska* investigates the 1908 Siberian explosion as a scientific "phenomenon," noting how the event "evolved into a phenomenon" producing "curious results" in culture and economy. Both treat the earth as a puzzle for international expert consensus; the Evenki people on whose land Tunguska fell, and whose accounts long predated the scientific expeditions, sit at the documentary's margins as local color rather than as knowers.

### *Do Rocks Dream of Flying?* (Argentina, 2025)

A poetic outlier: "Professor Pereda, an acoustic geologist," conducts geo-acoustic research on a volcanic island in the wake of eruption. The film's title — echoing the question of whether the inanimate dreams — gestures toward a non-extractive, almost animist relation to stone, listening to rather than mining the earth. It is the corpus's most explicit attempt to imagine geological attention as *attunement* rather than *appropriation*, and as such forms a quiet dialectical answer to *Where the Green Ants Dream*: a Western scientist's instruments reaching, however tentatively, toward the proposition that rock might have an interiority of its own.

### *Alice's Birthday* (Russia, 2009)

The science-fiction outlier substitutes an *archaeologist* and alien-planet "expedition" for terrestrial geology, displacing the entire frontier-expedition structure onto another world. It is worth noting only as evidence of how durable the expeditionary template is: the scientist-on-a-dead-planet, racing to undo a plague brought by a prior expedition, replays the colonial encounter — contamination, expertise, rescue — in interplanetary register, the alien world standing in for the always-already-emptied frontier.

## Synthesis: A Spatial Grammar of the Geologist Film

Reading the corpus together, a consistent grammar emerges.

**Verticality versus horizontality.** Geological knowledge is structurally *vertical* — it concerns depth, strata, what lies beneath. Local and Indigenous knowledge is structurally *horizontal* — it concerns relation, surface, dwelling, the spread of story across a territory. Films stage their central conflict as a collision between these two axes. The mining survey in *Green Ants*, the diamond map in *Letter Never Sent*, the dam survey in *A Place in the World* all assert the vertical, value-bearing column against the horizontal field of lived relation. The land becomes contested precisely because the geologist's gaze rotates it ninety degrees — from a place one is *on* and *of* to a resource one extracts *from below*.

**Knowledge as the instrument of alienation.** Across the extractive cluster, the geologist's product — the map, the survey, the feasibility report — is the document that makes land *alienable*. Before the survey, the valley is dwelt in; after it, it is dammable. The expertise does not merely describe the land; it performs the first legal-economic act of separating people from it. This is why so many of these films end in catastrophe (fire, collapse, tsunami, death): the narrative returns, as repressed, the violence that the calm language of survey conceals.

**The asymmetry of epistemic standing.** The films are unanimous, even when they don't intend to be, on one point: scientific knowledge of land is granted *transferable authority* — it counts in courts, capitals, and markets — while Indigenous and local knowledge is admitted only as belief, obstinacy, or folklore. The deep-time documentaries perform this asymmetry most insidiously, because they do it without an explicit antagonist: by framing land as a billion-year text legible only to the visiting expert, they render its actual inhabitants invisible without ever having to argue against them.

**Who benefits.** Tracing benefit across the corpus: private capital (the lumber baron, the patron, the mining company), the metropolitan state (Moscow, Prague), or the international scientific-cultural apparatus (the documentary, the museum, the ministry). The land's own people — when present at all — benefit in none of these films. The closest the corpus comes to an alternative is the listening posture of *Do Rocks Dream of Flying?* and the refusal-by-undoing of *Beyond Sleep*, both of which imagine a geology that does not convert the earth into someone else's asset.

**The productive silences.** Finally, the most politically charged feature of the corpus is *absence*. The Sámi in the Norwegian films, the Siberian peoples in *Letter Never Sent* and *Big Bang in Tunguska*, the Andean communities behind *Sacsayhuamán*, the Indigenous nations of the Grand Canyon — these are the knowers whose erasure the geologist film accomplishes most efficiently not by argument but by framing the ground as empty, ancient, hazardous, or merely "natural." To map the land as resource or as deep-time spectacle is, in every case, to map its prior knowers out of the frame.

## Coda

The geologist figure is so generative for a cultural geography of land precisely because the geologist's work sits at the seam where *knowing* becomes *taking*. The corpus, read as a whole, is a sustained meditation — sometimes critical, often unwitting — on how scientific authority over the subsurface becomes authority over the people on the surface. The richest films (*Where the Green Ants Dream*, *A Place in the World*, *The Skin of the South*) make this seam visible and let it tear. The rest, including the handsome recent deep-time documentaries, tend to suture it shut — offering the sublime of geological time as a substitute for the politics of geological power. The task of a critical reading is to keep the seam open: to ask, every time a geologist kneels to read a rock, *for whom is this ground being made legible, and at whose expense?*
