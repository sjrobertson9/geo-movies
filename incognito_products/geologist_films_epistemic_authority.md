# The Geologist on Screen: Epistemic Authority, Capital, and the Politics of Believing the Expert

*An analysis of seventeen films featuring geologist characters, read through Science and Technology Studies*

---

## Framing the problem

The geologist is an unusually instructive figure for studying public representations of expertise. Unlike the physician, whose authority is exercised on the individual body, or the physicist, whose knowledge is abstract and remote, the geologist speaks about the ground itself — the literal substrate on which communities are built, on which capital wants to build, and which the state wants to map, dam, mine, or settle. The geologist's claim is therefore always a claim against something: against a development scheme, a relocation, a settlement pattern, a profit margin. This makes the figure a natural site for dramatizing what STS calls the *credibility contest* — the social negotiation through which a knowledge-claim is granted or denied the status of authoritative fact.

What is striking across this corpus is how rarely the geologist's knowledge is simply *adopted*. Far more often it is heard and then overridden — not because it is wrong (the films almost always vindicate the science in the end) but because competing interests possess greater social power to determine which truths count. The recurring narrative engine is not ignorance but **the structured non-uptake of accurate knowledge**. The films, in other words, are less about whether science is correct than about whether being correct is enough. It usually is not.

I organize the analysis around three relationships through which the films position scientific expertise: expertise versus **capital**, expertise versus **the state**, and expertise versus **lay and indigenous knowledge**. A fourth section treats the documentary and "vindicatory" films, where the credibility contest is resolved in favor of science and the geologist is repositioned as a heroic witness rather than an ignored Cassandra.

## I. Expertise against capital: the Cassandra structure

The single most consistent pattern in this corpus is the geologist whose warning is materially correct and socially powerless because it threatens a profit. This is the Cassandra structure: prophecy that is true, disbelieved, and tragically borne out.

**The Skin of the South** (南国の肌, Japan, 1952) is the corpus's clearest statement of the pattern, and notable for its early date. A group of young geologists declares a mountainside marked for residential development unstable and is met with scorn from two directions at once — the villagers who refuse relocation and a lumber baron whose extraction will further destabilize the slope. The film stages epistemic authority as doubly besieged: the scientists possess accurate knowledge of the land but command neither the economic power of the baron nor the trust of the community whose homes are at stake. Their "pleas for reason" are not refuted; they are simply outweighed. The social consequence is catastrophe, and the film locates the cause not in scientific error but in the subordination of knowledge to capital and to a community's understandable resistance to displacement. That this appears in a 1952 Japanese film — in a society then acutely conscious of both natural catastrophe and reconstruction-era development pressure — suggests the geologist-as-ignored-Cassandra was a culturally available figure remarkably early.

**The Wave** (Bølgen, Norway, 2015) and **The Quake** (Skjelvet, Norway, 2018) modernize the structure into the disaster-thriller idiom. In *The Wave*, a mountain collapse above the Geiranger fjord produces a tsunami that, the overview stresses, was *theorised* — the knowledge existed — yet *no one is really ready*. The geologist is caught in the middle of an event his discipline had already conceptualized. The dramatic tension turns on the gap between institutional knowledge and institutional readiness: the science is sound, but the monitoring, warning, and evacuation apparatus that would convert knowledge into safety is shown to be slow, underfunded, or disbelieved until the ground actually moves. *The Quake* repeats the structure in Oslo. Read together, the two Norwegian films are interesting because the antagonist is no longer a named capitalist but a more diffuse social inertia — the tourism economy, the bureaucratic threshold of proof, the ordinary refusal to act on a low-probability/high-consequence forecast. The geologist's authority is genuine but activates only retroactively, after the catastrophe has supplied the missing political will. The consequence the films dramatize is that under late-capitalist risk governance, expertise is heard but not *acted upon* until acting is too late.

**A Place in the World** (Un lugar en el mundo, Argentina/Spain/Uruguay, 1992) inverts the valence and is the most politically pointed entry. Here the geologist — Hans, a "jaded Spanish geological engineer" — is not the ignored truth-teller but the *instrument of capital*. He arrives to survey whether the valley can be dammed for hydroelectric power, a project that would drive the peasants off the land and into the cities. The film thereby exposes the other face of geological expertise: it is not intrinsically on the side of the vulnerable. Knowledge of the land can be commissioned, and the surveyor's report becomes the technical pretext for dispossession. Epistemic authority here is openly aligned with *el patrón*, the local landowner-patron; the science is credible precisely because it serves power. The social consequence of *accepting* this expertise would be displacement. The film's sympathy lies with the idealistic cooperative and clinic that the dam would drown, positioning lay solidarity against expert-enabled capital. Hans's jadedness is the moral tell: the film knows that the same disciplinary competence can be a Cassandra in one film and a bulldozer in another, depending entirely on who pays for it.

The juxtaposition of *The Skin of the South* and *A Place in the World* is the analytic heart of the corpus. In the first, capital ignores the geologist and disaster follows; in the second, capital *hires* the geologist and dispossession follows. Expertise is shown to have no fixed political content. Its consequences depend on its patronage.

## II. Expertise and the state: mapping, mission, and the labor of legitimation

A second cluster positions the geologist not against capital but as an agent of the state's project of legibility — the surveying, mapping, and resource-cataloguing through which a state comes to know and control territory.

**Letter Never Sent** (Неотправленное письмо, USSR, 1960) is the canonical example and an ideologically loaded one. Four geologists search for diamonds in the Siberian wilderness; having found their deposit and put the mine "on the map," they must deliver that map to Moscow, only to be trapped by a catastrophic forest fire. The map is the film's central object: it is the conversion of raw nature into state-legible resource, and the geologists' lives become subordinate to its delivery. Here scientific expertise is fully fused with the state's developmental mission. There is no credibility contest — no one doubts the geologists — because in the Soviet frame the scientist *is* the heroic embodiment of collective purpose. The drama relocates from "will they be believed?" to "will the knowledge survive the indifference of nature?" The social meaning is sacrificial: the expert's body is expendable, the knowledge is not. This is epistemic authority absorbed entirely into state ideology, with the natural world cast as the only adversary.

**Focusing on the Turquoise Mountain** (Ve znamení Tyrkysové hory, Czechoslovakia/Mongolia, 1978) presents a gentler, comedic version of the same configuration: a Mongolian-Czech collaboration discovering mineral deposits, framed as fraternal socialist cooperation. The geologist here is an emissary of internationalist development, and the humorous register signals that the science is *unproblematic* — its authority is taken for granted and the only obstacles are logistical and cultural-comic. Expertise is legitimated not through conflict but through its frictionless service to a shared developmental ideal.

**Papaya: Love Goddess of the Cannibals** (Italy, 1978) sits at the lurid extreme of the state-and-capital nexus and deserves inclusion precisely because of how baldly it allegorizes. A team of geologists attempts to remove a native population from an island to perform atomic research; the island's "Cannibal" leader picks them off. Beneath the exploitation-horror surface is a recognizable colonial-extractive scenario: technical expertise (geology, atomic science) serving a removal project, met by violent indigenous refusal. The film grants the geologists no real epistemic authority in the narrative's moral economy — they are interlopers whose technical mission is the engine of dispossession, and their destruction is staged as a kind of retribution. However exploitative its execution, the film inverts the credibility hierarchy: the lay/indigenous body, not the expert, holds the film's (admittedly prurient) moral charge.

## III. Expertise against lay and indigenous knowledge: competing cosmologies

The richest STS material is in the films that stage a direct collision between geological expertise and an *alternative knowledge system* — not ignorance, but a rival way of knowing the land.

**Where the Green Ants Dream** (Wo die grünen Ameisen träumen, Australia/Germany, 1984) is the corpus's masterpiece of this theme and the film most directly about epistemic authority itself. Aboriginal Australians hold that a particular site is where the green ants go to dream, and that disturbing their dreams will bring catastrophe; a mining company wants to tear open the same soil to search for uranium. The film stages this not as superstition-versus-science but as *two incommensurable epistemologies competing for the same ground*. Crucially, the geological/mining expertise is granted full technical validity within its own frame — the company genuinely can find uranium — yet the film refuses to let that validity settle the question of authority. The Aboriginal claim cannot be translated into the evidentiary language the company and the courts recognize, and that untranslatability is the point. Epistemic authority is shown to be a matter of which language the institutions of capital and state are built to hear. Geological expertise wins not because it is truer but because the legal and economic order is already calibrated to its idiom. The social consequence is the slow erasure of a knowledge system that has no standing in the credibility contest because the contest's rules were written by the other side. Herzog turns the geologist's authority into an indictment of the institutional machinery that confers authority.

**Papaya** (above) belongs partly here too, as the crude exploitation-film cousin of Herzog's meditation: in both, technical expertise is the spearhead of removal and indigenous refusal is the counter-force, though *Green Ants* treats the collision with philosophical seriousness where *Papaya* treats it as spectacle.

**The Living Stones of Sacsayhuamán** (Живые камни Саксайуамана, Russia, 2014, documentary) introduces a subtler tension. Russian geophysicists conduct soil research at the Inca citadel at the request of Peru's Ministry of Culture, confronting a structure whose construction "no living person knows" how to explain. Here modern geoscientific instrumentation is deployed against the knowledge of a vanished civilization. The film positions expertise humbly — as a tool for *recovering* lost lay/ancestral knowledge rather than overriding it — though its framing flirts with the "ancient mystery" register that can quietly delegitimize indigenous engineering achievement by implying it requires exotic explanation. The credibility dynamic is inverted: the state (Peru's culture ministry) *commissions* the expertise, and the science serves heritage rather than extraction.

## IV. The interior and the absurd: expertise turned inward

Two films detach the geologist from any social credibility contest and relocate the drama inside the expert's own mind, which is itself a revealing move.

**Beyond Sleep** (Aldri sove mer, Norway, 2016) follows a young geologist into the northern tundra to finish research on meteorite craters; he gets "hopelessly lost in both the wilderness and his own mind." Here the question is not whether others believe the geologist but whether the geologist can sustain the disciplined perception that expertise requires when the environment overwhelms the apparatus of objective observation. The film privatizes the credibility contest: the threat to knowledge is internal — exhaustion, disorientation, the collapse of the observing self. Read through STS, it dramatizes the embodied, fragile labor that the polished authority of the published result conceals.

**Alice's Birthday** (День рождения Алисы, Russia, 2009, animation/sci-fi) is a children's film, but its scientist-explorers (an archaeologist and alien researchers) embody a wholly *trusted* expertise: knowledge is adventurous, benevolent, and unquestioned, deployed to save a plague-destroyed planet. It belongs to the optimistic-pedagogical tradition that presents science to children as reliable heroism — the opposite pole from the besieged Cassandras of the disaster films. The contrast matters: the figure of the *ignored* expert is largely absent from films made to socialize children *into* trust in science.

**Do Rocks Dream of Flying?** (Argentina, 2025, sci-fi/documentary short) gives us Professor Pereda, an "acoustic geologist" conducting geo-acoustic research on an island devastated by volcanic eruption. The hybrid documentary-fiction form and the invented sub-specialty signal a contemporary, poetic relation to expertise: the geologist as a listener to the planet, a figure of attunement rather than authority or warning. The title's echo of "Do Androids Dream...?" hints at a meditation on what it means to attribute interiority — dreaming, flying — to the inert mineral world the geologist studies. Authority here is neither contested nor commissioned; it is reframed as a contemplative, almost artistic mode of attention.

## V. The vindicatory documentaries: expertise as heroic witness

Several entries are documentaries in which the credibility contest is already resolved in science's favor, and the geologist appears as authoritative guide and adventurer rather than embattled prophet.

**Grand Canyon — A Journey to the Center of the Earth** (Grand Canyon, France, 2025) foregrounds geologists Karl Karlström and Laurie Crossey as "the leading experts" who descend the Colorado over eight days through hundreds of rapids to read two billion years of Earth history in the rock. Expertise here is embodied, physical, heroic — authority earned through bodily risk and granted unquestioningly by the film's frame. **Great Natural Monuments — Iceland** (Islande, la quête des origines, France/Switzerland, 2023) similarly follows three researchers through Iceland's volcanic zones as a "natural laboratory," and **Voyages au centre de la Terre: Dans les pas de Jules Verne** (France, 2023) and **Big Bang in Tunguska** (Das Rätsel von Tunguska, Germany, 2008) extend the pattern — the latter notably foregrounding *unresolved* scientific controversy, with renowned scientists still arguing about the cause of the 1908 explosion. *Tunguska* is the most epistemologically honest of the documentaries: it presents expertise as contested *within* science, refusing the heroic-guide closure and showing authority as an ongoing argument rather than a settled verdict.

**Diplom-Geologe / Diplom-Geologin** (Germany, 1990) — an eight-minute profile of the profession itself — and the documentaries generally perform a *legitimation* function: they construct the geologist as a credible, admirable knower for a public audience. Where the fiction films dramatize the *failure* of credibility, the documentaries manufacture it.

## Synthesis: what believing the geologist is really about

Reading the corpus together, a structure emerges that is more sociological than scientific. Across the fiction films, the geologist is almost never *wrong*. The drama is never epistemic in the narrow sense — it is not about whether the science is correct. The drama is about **the social conditions under which correct knowledge is permitted to matter**. Four findings stand out.

First, **accuracy is decoupled from authority**. The geologist is repeatedly vindicated by events (the mountain falls, the tsunami comes, the deposit is real) yet repeatedly fails to convert that accuracy into uptake. Films from *The Skin of the South* (1952) to *The Wave* (2015) share this structure across six decades and three continents, suggesting it expresses something durable about how modern publics relate to expert warning: we believe the expert only after the catastrophe certifies him.

Second, **expertise has no fixed political valence**. The same disciplinary competence is a martyred truth-teller in *The Skin of the South*, a sacrificial state hero in *Letter Never Sent*, and the surveyor of dispossession in *A Place in the World* and *Papaya*. Whether geological knowledge protects or harms the vulnerable depends entirely on its patronage — who commissions it and whose interests it is made to serve. The films are, collectively, an argument that the politics of expertise lie in its funding and institutional position, not in the knowledge itself.

Third, **capital and the state set the terms of the credibility contest**. The geologist loses to the lumber baron and the developer not on the merits but because economic interest controls the threshold of actionable proof; the geologist wins against indigenous knowledge in *Green Ants* not on the merits but because the legal-economic order speaks his language and not the Aborigines'. Authority, in these films, is conferred by the institution that adjudicates the contest, and that institution is rarely neutral.

Fourth, **the documentary and children's films perform the inverse labor** — actively constructing the geologist as trustworthy, heroic, and benevolent. The culture both dramatizes the *failure* to believe experts (in its disaster fictions) and works to *produce* belief in them (in its documentaries and pedagogy). The geologist on screen is thus a site where a society argues with itself about whether, and on whose authority, to trust those who claim to know the ground beneath its feet.

The corpus's deepest lesson is that the question "is the geologist believed?" is always shadowed by the prior question "*believed by whom, and who gets to decide?*" The films that understand this — *Where the Green Ants Dream*, *A Place in the World*, *The Skin of the South*, *Big Bang in Tunguska* — are the ones that grasp that epistemic authority is not a property of knowledge but a settlement of power.
